The CD Player

May 2026

I was twelve years old when I learned that protection is a lie we tell older siblings to make them feel useful.

You're handed this small, fragile, crying thing and somewhere between the first diaper change and the first argument over the remote control, it becomes your person. Not because anyone told you to make them your person. Just because they were there, every day, in the same house, breathing the same air, watching the same movies until the lines became scripture.

Geeti and I watched Kuch Kuch Hota Hai so many times we stopped watching it and started reciting it. It wasn't a movie anymore. It was a language we shared. A private frequency. She had a CD player — Himesh Reshammiya's greatest hits, always in the tray, ready to go. If you know, you know. If you don't, just understand that this man's voice was everywhere in that era, inescapable, the soundtrack to an entire generation of South Asian childhoods. She loved him with a conviction I respected even then.

That CD player is the thing that gets me. Not the big moments. The small, stupid, mundane object sitting on a shelf somewhere in my memory, Himesh still loaded, ready to play for a girl who isn't there anymore.

She was sixteen. I was twelve. She had meningitis that nobody in Afghanistan had diagnosed yet, because that was the reality of where we were and what we had. The last time I saw her, the entire left side of her body was failing. Shaking. Giving out on her in real time. She was sixteen and her body was filing for bankruptcy and none of us had the language for what we were watching.

I didn't protect her. That's the thing older siblings carry that nobody warns you about. The completely irrational, totally consuming, factually incorrect belief that you were supposed to stop it. That your one job was to keep this person safe and you failed at the only thing that mattered. Nobody told me this was my job. No parent sat me down and assigned me the task. It just arrived, fully formed, the moment she didn't.

Then came Chacho.

My uncle. My dad's only brother. He'd spent most of his young adult life in London, which in Afghanistan at that time meant he walked into every room carrying a different kind of gravity. London swagger is real, and his was effortless. He was the funniest person in any space he occupied, always, without trying. The most observant. The most understanding. The kind of man who made you feel like the most important person in the room just by actually listening to what you said.

He could sing. He could play keyboard in a way that made you want to rearrange your entire sense of what was possible for yourself. I started singing because of him. I wanted to be him so badly it wasn't even admiration anymore, it was aspiration at a molecular level. He was my hero in the truest sense of the word, not the poster-on-the-wall kind, the kind you actually try to become.

After Geeti died, he was all I had left that made sense.

He came back from London because he missed his big brother. Missed us. Missed Geeti, Shahram, me. He chose family over the life he had built, which at thirteen I didn't fully understand but filed away somewhere important.

A year after Geeti, he was gone too.

I was thirteen years old and I had now lost my sister and my grief companion in consecutive years. The person who helped me survive the first loss didn't survive long enough to see me come out the other side of it. That's not just loss. That's loss with the safety net cut. That's falling and looking down and realizing the person who was supposed to catch you is also falling.

People get weird about death. Especially when you're casual about it, or worse, when you make jokes. They flinch. They do the head tilt. They say things like "she's in a better place" or "he's watching over you" with a confidence that I find both touching and completely insane.

I was not raised to believe in the ballpark in the sky. I don't think Geeti is somewhere waiting for me. I don't think Chacho is watching me from a cloud, keyboard in hand, probably killing it at open mic night in paradise. I think they're gone in every way except memory, and I think that's both the saddest and the most clarifying thing I've ever had to sit with.

What I do know is that the only truly helpful thing you can say to someone who is grieving is some version of: "That's absolutely terrible and I'm not going to pretend otherwise." That's it. No fixing. No reframing. No pivoting to the silver lining. Just sitting down in the wreckage with someone and acknowledging that yes, this is wreckage.

Grief vultures are real. The ones who race to tell you they feel the worst about it, that they miss them too, that they know exactly what you're going through. You don't. You can be sad that I'm sad. That's valid and I appreciate it. But this grief is mine. I'll cry you under the table. Back up.

Here's the part I don't talk about enough.

After you lose someone, the fear doesn't go away. It mutates. It finds new hosts.

I look at Shahram and Ilham, my younger brothers, and sometimes I can't breathe with how much I need them to be okay. I look at my parents and I feel the clock in a way no person my age should be this fluent in. My cousin Beheshta was there for me through all of it in ways I didn't have the wherewithal to recognize at the time, quietly showing up, consistently present, and I nearly missed it entirely because grief makes you temporarily blind to the people who are still standing.

The fear of losing the people I still have doesn't make me hold them closer. Not always. Sometimes it does the opposite. Sometimes it makes the distance feel safer. If you don't fully lean in, the fall is shorter. That's the lie you tell yourself at thirteen and sometimes forget to stop telling yourself later.

But then Himesh Reshammiya comes on somewhere, in a restaurant, on someone's phone, bleeding out of a passing car, and the CD player comes back. And Geeti comes back. And Chacho comes back. And the distance collapses and you remember that the whole point of loving people is that it costs you something when they go.

That's not a reason not to love them.

That's the whole reason.
