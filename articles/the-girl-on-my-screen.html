The Girl on My Screen

May 2026

I didn't mean to find her.

That's the thing about doomscrolling — you never mean to find anything. You're not looking for something specific, you're just moving. Thumb up, thumb up, thumb up. It's less browsing and more controlled falling. You're not searching, you're just hoping the algorithm throws you something worth stopping for.

Most nights it doesn't. Most nights it's the same recycled content dressed in different faces. But then, every once in a while, the scroll stops you cold.

That's what happened the night I came across a video of a girl I recognized.

Not her, exactly. I recognized the family. I'd known her older sibling, knew the household, knew the kind of home she came from. Conservative. Traditional. The kind of family where appearances weren't just kept up, they were inherited, ironed into you from birth. You know the type. Sunday clothes on a Wednesday. Shoes by the door. A grandmother's eyes in the back of every room.

And here was this girl, dancing seductively for a camera, for strangers, for an audience she'd never meet, performing for the approval of people who would move on in four seconds regardless of what she did.

I kept scrolling. Then I stopped. Then I went back.

Not for the reason you might think. I went back because I couldn't shake the image of her mother.

I don't know her mother personally. But I know enough to imagine her. I know what it looks like when a woman from that world sits down with a cup of tea she's been reheating for three hours, opens her phone with the careful attention of someone who's never fully trusted technology, and stumbles across her daughter doing exactly this.

What do you feel in that moment?

Is it rage? Probably. But before the rage — embarrassment? Grief? A specific kind of horror that doesn't have a clean name, the kind where you see your own life's sacrifices reflected back at you in a way you never imagined possible?

You raised a child. You made choices, maybe hard ones, maybe ones that cost you something real, to give that child a foundation. And now she's here, in a rectangle, giving herself away in four-second increments to men who'll never say thank you and will absolutely never call.

Is this what you pictured? When you held her as a baby, when you argued about her curfew, when you bought her first laptop, did you picture this?

Here's where it gets uncomfortable.

My brain, doing what brains do when they're allowed to wander, pulled out the Hitler question. You know the one. The philosophers' favorite party trick: if you could go back in time and kill Hitler as a baby, would you?

Bear with me.

Because the version I kept thinking about wasn't the Hitler one. It was quieter, and somehow more devastating. It was: if that mother had known, really known, how this specific chapter would turn out, would she have made different choices? Would she have had children at all?

And I sat with that question for longer than was probably healthy.

Because the honest answer isn't yes or no. The honest answer is: that's the wrong question, and we keep asking it anyway.

No child arrives broken. No child is born already performing for strangers, already hungry for the wrong kind of attention, already running the calculus of likes and views and comments before they've figured out who they actually are. That's not how any of this starts. Every girl on every screen was once a child in someone's house, being shaped slowly, invisibly, constantly by everything around her.

By what was said. By what was never said. By what was rewarded and what was punished and what was quietly tolerated. By the screen time. By the friend groups. By the way the adults in her life talked about women when they thought nobody was listening. By the culture. By the algorithm. By us — the people who stop scrolling long enough to watch.

So no. You couldn't have killed the Hitler baby, because the Hitler baby wasn't Hitler yet. And no, that mother couldn't have predicted this, because the girl in the video isn't just a product of one household's choices. She's a product of an entire ecosystem, one we all live inside of, one we're all feeding, every time we open the app.

That's the part that made me put my phone down.

Not the girl. Not the mother. The algorithm.

Because the algorithm didn't create this girl. But it found her. It found exactly what she needed: attention, visibility, the dopamine hit of a number going up, and it handed her a stage. And the stage came with an audience that doesn't care about her, can't remember her name by the next video, and will quietly tune out the moment she stops performing.

And on the other side of the screen, the rest of us are scrolling. Just passing through. Just watching. Handing her the views, feeding the machine, then moving on and feeling vaguely bad about it later.

I doomscrolled into something I didn't want to find, and then I sat with it long enough to realize the discomfort wasn't really about her.

It was about what it means to exist in a world where a platform this powerful has no memory, no care, and no conscience — and we keep using it anyway. Every night. Thumb up. Thumb up. Thumb up.

The scroll doesn't stop for anyone.

But maybe, occasionally, we should.
